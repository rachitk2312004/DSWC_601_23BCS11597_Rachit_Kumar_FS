Day3 Q2 ANSWER — MediCore Emergency Triage System
===================================================
Concepts: Comparable<T>, PriorityQueue, Multi-Tier Sorting,
          Binary Heap, O(log N) Insertion


WHAT'S THE BIG IDEA HERE?
--------------------------
Two things are being tested here.

First: can you write clean multi-field sorting logic? Most people get messy
with deeply nested if/else blocks when sorting by multiple criteria.
The clean approach is: compare the primary field first, return immediately
if they differ, only fall through to the secondary field if needed.
Long.compare() and enum.compareTo() make both comparisons a single line each.

Second: do you understand WHY a PriorityQueue is better than a sorted List
for a system that receives continuous input?


WHY IMPLEMENT Comparable AND NOT JUST USE A Comparator?
---------------------------------------------------------
Both work, but Comparable makes more sense here because this priority ordering
IS the natural ordering of patients in a triage context. There's no alternate
interpretation — a CRITICAL patient always goes before a STABLE one. Period.

Comparable says "this is how I naturally compare to other objects of my type."
Comparator says "here's a custom, one-off way to order these objects."

When the ordering is inherent to the domain model, Comparable is the right call.


THE TWO-TIER SORT EXPLAINED
-----------------------------
We need two rules:
  Rule 1 — severity: CRITICAL before URGENT before STABLE.
  Rule 2 — if tied on severity, whoever arrived first goes first.

Enums have ordinals: CRITICAL=0, URGENT=1, STABLE=2.
The PriorityQueue is a min-heap — smallest value has highest priority.
So lower ordinal = higher priority. That's exactly what we want.

  this.severity.compareTo(other.severity)

This is just comparing ordinals. If this patient is CRITICAL (0) and the
other is STABLE (2), we get 0 - 2 = negative. Negative means "this first."

If they have the same severity, compareTo returns 0 and we fall through to:

  Long.compare(this.arrivalTime, other.arrivalTime)

Earlier arrival = smaller timestamp = negative result = "this first."
Again exactly what we want.

The whole compareTo() method ends up being about 4 lines. No nested ifs.


WHY PRIORITYQUEUE BEATS A SORTED LIST HERE
--------------------------------------------
Imagine patients keep arriving all day — hundreds per hour.

With a sorted List:
  Every time a new patient arrives, you'd call Collections.sort() again.
  That's O(N log N) per admission. As N grows, this gets very slow.

With a PriorityQueue (binary min-heap):
  Inserting a new patient takes O(log N). The heap just bubbles the new
  patient up until it finds the right spot. That's at most log(N) comparisons.
  Getting the next patient to treat is O(log N) for poll() (re-heapify after removal).
  Peeking at who's next (without removing) is O(1) — it's always the root node.

For a live intake system where patients arrive continuously, O(log N) per
insertion is far more sustainable than O(N log N) per sort.


HOW THE HEAP ORDERS OUR PATIENTS
----------------------------------
After admitting: Alice(STABLE/100), Bob(CRITICAL/200), Carol(URGENT/150),
                 Dan(CRITICAL/120), Eve(URGENT/300), Frank(STABLE/80)

The min-heap root is always the "smallest" per our compareTo():
  Root → Dan (CRITICAL, earliest CRITICAL arrival)

When Dan is polled and removed:
  Root → Bob (CRITICAL, next earliest CRITICAL)

When Bob is polled:
  Root → Carol (URGENT, earliest URGENT arrival)

...and so on. The heap re-balances after each removal in O(log N).


SAMPLE OUTPUT
--------------
===== MASS CASUALTY INTAKE =====

[ADMITTED] Alice [STABLE, arrived at T+100ms]
[ADMITTED] Bob [CRITICAL, arrived at T+200ms]
[ADMITTED] Carol [URGENT, arrived at T+150ms]
[ADMITTED] Dan [CRITICAL, arrived at T+120ms]
[ADMITTED] Eve [URGENT, arrived at T+300ms]
[ADMITTED] Frank [STABLE, arrived at T+80ms]

===== TREATMENT ORDER =====

[TREATING] Dan [CRITICAL, arrived at T+120ms]
[TREATING] Bob [CRITICAL, arrived at T+200ms]
[TREATING] Carol [URGENT, arrived at T+150ms]
[TREATING] Eve [URGENT, arrived at T+300ms]
[TREATING] Frank [STABLE, arrived at T+80ms]
[TREATING] Alice [STABLE, arrived at T+100ms]

Notice: Frank (STABLE, T+80) arrived before Alice (STABLE, T+100) AND before
most other patients. But he still waits behind all CRITICAL and URGENT patients.
Severity wins Tier 1. Arrival time only breaks ties within the same severity.
